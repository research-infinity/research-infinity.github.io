history of infinity

Ancient Greece


The Greeks viewed infinity negatively. The word range from many different meanings: unbound, indefinite, complex, total disorder, or undefined. The chaos before the world was created was considered infinite, a crooked line too. Aristotle, a Greek philosopher, noticed that many aspects of the world seemed infinite. Time could go on forever and space could be infinitly divisable, making it possible for a line to contain an infinite number of points. In order to avoid chaos and confusion that could threaten the orderliness of a finite universe, Aristotle coined the term potentially infinite; this way he did not have to consider actually infinte concepts and numbers. For example, he said that the set of all natural numbers is potentially infinite. Because there is no largest natural number and no finished list, the set of all natural numbers cannot be actually infinite. Plato and Pythagoras believed did not belive in any infinity, not even potential infinities. The fist philosopher to belive in the sightest concept of infinity was Plotinas; he belived that God, or the One, was infinite.


Absolutely One, it has never known measure and stands outside of number, and so is under no limit either in regard to anything external or internal, for any such determination would bring something of the dual into it. - Plotinas
